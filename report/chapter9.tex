\chapter{Conclusion}
\label{chap:conclusion}

\section{Summary of Achievements}

WaveGuard~V4 has successfully demonstrated the feasibility of a low-cost, autonomous
near-shore wave early-warning system suitable for the Kanayannur coastal zone of Kerala.
The following objectives were met:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item An ESP32-based IoT buoy integrating an MPU6050 accelerometer and NEO-6M GPS
        was designed, assembled, and calibrated for deployment in 5--10~m water depth
        at 10.05\textdegree{}N, 76.18\textdegree{}E.

  \item A three-tier RMS-threshold classifier (\textsc{calm} / \textsc{moderate} /
        \textsc{high-wave}) with calibration thresholds of 0.03~g and 0.075~g was
        implemented and validated with zero mis-classifications during pool testing.

  \item Dual-path alert delivery was realised: a React web dashboard (WiFi, $<$500~ms)
        and GSM SMS (SIM800L, 8--12~s) operate simultaneously on a HIGH\_WAVE event.

  \item The FastAPI + SQLite backend persisted all sensor readings reliably throughout
        a 6.4-hour prototype test; the POST success rate was 99.7\%.

  \item The entire bill of materials cost under \INR{}3\,200, confirming the
        economic viability of community-scale deployment.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Problem Addressed}

The project directly addresses the absence of affordable near-shore warning
infrastructure for the \textit{Kallakkadal} phenomenon in coastal Kerala.
Conventional NIOT deep-water buoys and satellite wave models do not resolve the
hyper-local shallow-water amplification that causes loss of life in the 1--5~km
near-shore zone during monsoon swell events.
WaveGuard~V4 fills this gap with a unit that can be manufactured locally, maintained
by non-specialist personnel, and powered from a single lithium cell for a full working day.

\section{Technical Innovations}

Three key technical contributions distinguish WaveGuard~V4:

\begin{description}
  \item[Dual-communication resilience:]
        Independent WiFi (LAN) and GSM (cellular) alert paths ensure that an alert
        is delivered even if one channel is unavailable---critical in the intermittent
        connectivity environment of the Kanayannur coastal zone.

  \item[Sensor fusion and calibration:]
        Tri-axial accelerometry combined with gravitational bias removal and a 50-Hz
        sampling regime produces wave metrics that correlate well ($\pm$0.006~g) with
        a reference instrument, demonstrating that commodity MEMS sensors are
        adequate for near-shore wave monitoring.

  \item[Low-power embedded design:]
        Careful power rail partitioning (direct cell feed to SIM800L; boost converter
        for ESP32; bulk decoupling capacitors) resolved all power-integrity failures
        and achieved the 6--8~hour runtime target, a critical field-deployment
        parameter.
\end{description}

\section{Impact on Coastal Safety}

If deployed as a network of 5 buoys spaced 1~km apart in the Kanayannur near-shore
zone, WaveGuard~V4 could provide 5--10~minute advance warning of incoming swell
surges---sufficient time for fishing boats within radio range to return to harbour.
The SMS channel reaches fisherfolk and coastal residents without requiring
smartphone ownership or internet access, aligning with the community's actual
technology profile.

\section{Limitations and Lessons Learned}

\begin{itemize}
  \item The SIM800L current surge issue (\S\,5.8) consumed significant debugging
        time and highlights the importance of consulting module datasheets for
        worst-case power figures before designing the power supply.
  \item The 50~m WiFi range limits the shore-station link to near-beach anchoring;
        cellular data upload (SIM800L TCP/IP) will be the primary channel in the
        next version.
  \item A single buoy cannot distinguish boat wake from ocean swell; the ML
        enhancement described in Chapter~\ref{chap:future} is therefore a
        high-priority next step.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

WaveGuard~V4 demonstrates that a system costing under \INR{}3\,500, built from
globally available commercial components, and running open-source software can
provide meaningful coastal wave monitoring capability at community scale.
The prototype meets all stated functional and non-functional requirements, and its
limitations are well understood and addressable in future iterations.
It is the authors' hope that this work contributes to the broader goal of protecting
the lives and livelihoods of Kerala's coastal fishing communities from the
ever-present threat of \textit{Kallakkadal}.
